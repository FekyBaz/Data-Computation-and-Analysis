Theoretical Differences & Analysis

Pearson Correlation

  • Assumes a linear relationship between continuous features and class labels.
  • Highly sensitive to outliers.
  • Pros: Very fast and simple for predominantly linear dependencies.
  • Cons: Misses complex, non-linear dependencies.

Spearman Rank Correlation

  • Measures monotonic relationships through ranks.
  • Robust to extreme outliers because it only acts on the relative rank order instead of raw distances.

Gini Index / Impurity

  • Evaluates how often a randomly chosen element would be incorrectly labeled.
  • Favors continuous features with a large number of distinct outcomes (binning is necessary).
  • Excellent for finding local partitions and highly prevalent in Decision Trees.






Chi-Square Statistic

  • Compares observed frequencies with theoretically expected ones showing statistical independence.
  • Requires categorical / binned data and relies strictly on distribution counts.
  • Assumes large enough frequencies per bin for statistical significance.

Information Gain (Entropy-based)

  • Represents the reduction in entropy (uncertainty) achieved by knowing a feature's value.
  • Inherently unbiased toward pure categorical distributions.
  • Tends to capture complex non-linear structures highly effectively compared to linear tests.
Summary Conclusion:
Pearson and Spearman evaluate global linear/monotonic trends. Conversely, Information Gain, Chi-Square, and Gini natively look at structural dependencies via entropy and outcome frequencies, making them suitable when relationships are non-linear or distributions are localized in distinct spaces. This explains the observed performance gap favoring Entropy/Gini methods for this dataset.
